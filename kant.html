Immanuel Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) was a German philosopher who is considered one of the most influential thinkers in the history of Western philosophy. His work synthesized rationalism and empiricism, laying the foundations for much of modern philosophy.

Life

Kant was born in Königsberg, Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia). He spent his entire life in Königsberg, working as a lecturer and professor at the University of Königsberg. Despite never traveling far, his ideas had a profound impact on global thought.

Philosophy

Kant's philosophy is divided into three main periods: the pre-Critical, Critical, and post-Critical. His most famous work, Critique of Pure Reason, explores the limits of human understanding and the structure of knowledge.

He distinguished between phenomena (things as they appear to us) and noumena (things as they are in themselves). Kant's ethical theory, deontology, emphasizes duty and universal moral laws, famously encapsulated in the categorical imperative.

Key Works

  • Critique of Pure Reason (1781/1787)
  • Critique of Practical Reason (1788)
  • Critique of Judgment (1790)
  • Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals (1785)
