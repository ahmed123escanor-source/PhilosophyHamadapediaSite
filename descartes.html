René Descartes

René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist, widely considered the father of modern philosophy. He is best known for his philosophical statement "Cogito, ergo sum" (I think, therefore I am), which appears in his seminal work Meditations on First Philosophy.

Life

Descartes was born in La Haye en Touraine (now Descartes, Indre-et-Loire), France. He studied at the Jesuit Collège Royal Henry-Le-Grand in La Flèche. After completing his education, he traveled extensively across Europe, serving in various armies and engaging in scientific and philosophical pursuits.

Philosophy

Descartes' philosophy is foundational to modern Western thought. He sought to establish a firm foundation for knowledge by doubting all beliefs that could be doubted. In his method of doubt, he concluded that the only indubitable truth is his own existence as a thinking being.

His dualism posits a clear distinction between mind (res cogitans) and body (res extensa), influencing subsequent philosophy and science.

Contributions to Mathematics and Science

Descartes is also credited with the development of analytic geometry, linking algebra and geometry. His work in optics and physiology further advanced scientific understanding.

Key Works

  • Meditations on First Philosophy (1641)
  • Principles of Philosophy (1644)
